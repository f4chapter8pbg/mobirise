Product & Service Management as a Value-Creation Process

Products and services are the key sources of customer value. The management aims to optimize the value that a company’s products and services deliver to target customers and do so in a way that benefits the company and its collaborators.
P&S are closely related but with several important differences that distinguish products from services. 
Product

Products typically change ownership during purchase, they can be physically separated from the manufacturer and distributed to end users via multiple channels.

Products are often classified as either durable goods e.g. cars and home appliances; or non-durable goods, such as food, cosmetics and so on. 

Services

Usually do not involve a change in ownership, the customer acquires the right to use the offering and receive its benefits within a given time frame.

Usually delivered and consumed at the same time. The inseparability makes services difficult to standardize, and their quality varies across service occasions depending on: The type of customer; The particular service provider; The interaction between the provider and the customer.

Inseparability also makes services difficult to inventory, which means some industries that have a fixed service capacity face fluctuating customer demand.

P&S are two of seven attributes defining the company’s offerings. In concert with other marketing tactics, P&S aim to create an optimal value proposition (OVP) for the company, its customers, and its collaborators.

Therefore, the ultimate goal of the management is to design P&S that create superior value for target customers in a way that benefits the company and its collaborators.

Dimensions of Product and Service Experiences

Based on the level of uncertainty associated with their performance, product and service attributes can be classified into one of three categories: search, experience, and credence.
Search

associated with the least amount of uncertainty
typically identifiable through inspection before purchase .

e.g. the color of a car,  
the size and shape of a toothpaste tube,  
the type of cuisine offered by a restaurant 

Experience

carry greater uncertainty
revealed only through consumption.

e.g. the comfort of a car, 
the flavor of a toothpaste, 
the taste of a meal in a restaurant 

Credence

have the greatest amount of uncertainty. Quality is not revealed even after consumption

e.g. the safety of a car, 
the cavity prevention benefits of a toothpaste, 
the calorie count of a restaurant meal 

In general, search attributes are more common for tangible offerings, whereas credence attributes are more typical for intangible offerings.


The distinction between search, experience, and credence attributes is important because it determines the way the product and service aspects of the offering should be communicated to potential buyers.   

  1. Communicating search attributes is relatively straightforward and merely involves informing target customers about the properties of the company’s products and services.
  2. Communicating experience attributes is more complex. Products and services rich in experience attributes can benefit from product samples and free trials so that customers can appreciate the benefits of the offering.
  3. Communicating credence attributes calls for ensuring that customers trust the company’s promises. Such trust can be achieved by building a reputable brand that can support the company’s claims about the unobservable performance of its products and services.

Key Product and Service Decisions

Performance

Products vary in their performance on different attributes. Key principle in deciding level of performance of a given offering is optimizing its value for relevant market entities.

Consistency

Consistency is of vital importance in product and service delivery. 
Six-sigma approach - 3.4 defective items per million outcomes.

Reliability

Refers to the probability that the product or service will operate according to its specifications for the duration of its projected life cycle.

Durability

Products that are more durable tend to be preferred by customers, but might have negative impact on company. Planned Obsolescence is the process of designing new products that makes prios generations inferior.

Compatibility

Is the product or service consistent with certain already existing standards?

Ease of Use

Common misconception -Greater functionality and greater number of features leads to greater satisfaction.

Technological Design

Two development methods: product innovation, product variation  
Product innovation involves technology-based innovations and innovative use of existing technology to design new offering 
Product variation leads to offerings characterized by relatively minor variations I their functionality. 

Degree of Customization

Two extremes: one for all and one for one  
Most products use mass-production strategy to service all customers 
  

Form

All about physical aspects of the offering, such as its size and shape

Style

The look and feel of an offering, important for products because it has a primarily pleasant and self- expressive function. 
Style can create value above and beyond the functional characteristics.
